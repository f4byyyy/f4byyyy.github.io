This article/guide is for you if you are already familiar with the basics of photography and want get a deeper understanding of the workings of your camera and the differences between cameras with e.g. different sensor sizes.

Equivalence

This section might help you to understand the real difference between differently sized camera sensors.

Crop Factor

First we have to understand what crop factor (CF) means. The crop factor of a sensor is the ratio of the diagonal of a full frame sensor to its diagonal:

$ \text{CF} = \displaystyle\frac{\text{diag}_{FF}}{\text{diag}_{Sensor}} $

Of course we can also use a different sensor size than full frame as the base size for the crop factor, but using the full frame diagonal is most common.

Note that if the sensor in question has the same aspect ratio as the full frame sensor (which is 3:2), then the crop factor is simply the number you multiply the sensor's width and height by to get the full frame sensor's width and height.

Example: Let's take a Micro Four Thirds sensor. Its dimensions are 17.3 mm × 13 mm whereas a full frame sensor measures 36 mm × 24 mm. The diagonal of the MFT sensor is 21.64 mm and the diagonal of a FF sensor measures 43.267 mm. So the resulting crop factor is 2.

Calculating Equivalent Values

Now with that sorted out we can take a look at the formulas for calculating the full frame equivalent of different variables:

$ \text{Equivalent Focal Length} = \text{(Focal Length)} \times \text{(Crop Factor)} $

$ \text{Equivalent Aperture} = \text{(F-Number)} \times \text{(Crop Factor)} $

$ \text{Equivalent ISO} = \text{(ISO)} \times \text{(Crop Factor)}^2 $

Now what does equivalence mean? If you set up two cameras with differently sized sensors with equivalent settings you will essentially get the same picture. Things like brightness, depth of field and field of view are the same. And even the noise level and dynamic range should be (roughly) the same, at least if the two sensors have comparable performance (i.e. they were produced in the same era). The only differences of the two pictures might lie in the color science of the cameras or some properties of the different lenses like chromatic aberration. Obviously you also have to position both camreas identically to caputre the same image.

Example 1: Let's take a camera with a full frame sensor and one with an APS-C sensor. The crop factor of a typical APS-C sensor is 43.267 / 28.4 = 1.523 (Some APS-C sensors have a slightly different size: e.g. Canon's APS-C sensors have a crop factor of 1.6). Let's say on our APS-C camera we have a 50 mm lens at f/2.8 and set ISO to 400 and the shutter speed to 1/125. Now the equivalent variables on our full frame camera would be:

  • Focal length: 50 mm × 1.523 = 76.15 mm
  • Aperture: 2.8 × 1.523 = f/4.264
  • ISO: 400 × 1.523² = 927.81
  • Shutter speed: 1/125

What happens if you only change some of the variables to be equivalent?

  • An equivalent focal length gives the same field of view
  • An equivalent aperture gives the same depth of field (given an equivalent focal length)
  • An equivalent ISO gives (roughly) the same noise level and dynamic range if the sensors have comparable performance. It also gives the same brightness if the aperture is equivalent and the shutter speed matches

Full Frame vs. APS-C Sensor

As detailed in the section about equivalence an APS-C sensor only really has two (small) downsides compared to a full frame sensor: First of all the dynamic range and noise at base ISO is slightly worse. Apart from that you can use a faster lens on an APS-C camera to get the same results as with a full frame camera, as long as a faster lens is available. This shows the second downside: Since you probably won't find a faster lens than f/0.95 for a regular APS-C camera, a full frame camera will also have an advantage if you pair it with a lens faster than f/1.425 (= 0.95 × 1.5) since there will be no equivalent lens available for APS-C.

However, there is more to consider than the image you will get: Price and weight are also important factors when choosing a camera and lens. In theory an APS-C lens can be a bit smaller than an equivalent full frame lens. This could make an equivalent APS-C lens slightly cheaper and lighter but in practise this is not always the case since the quality of the lens also plays a big role here. Also an APS-C camera might be a bit cheaper than a comparable full frame model.

Depth of Field

Depth of field is the length of the area that is acceptably sharp. It is affected by three factors:

  • Aperture: wider aperture gives shallower depth of field
  • Distance to subject: focusing closer gives shallower depth of field
  • Focal length: A longer focal lenght gives shallower depth of field

If you change focal length and adjust your position so that the subject stays the same size, depth of field will actually stay the same, but a longer focal length will still give you more background blur since the background is enlarged compared to a shorter focal length. Technically defining what "acceptably sharp" means also has an effect on depth of field, but we will ignore that here. The website dofsimulator.net is great if you want to see the effects of changing the different factors.
